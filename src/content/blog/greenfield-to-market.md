Building a manufacturing plant is an engineering challenge. Turning it into a profitable business is a commercial one. Having led the commercialization of Archit Nuwood's 360 CBM/day MDF plant as National Business Head, I can say the second challenge is harder — and less understood.

## The Core Problem: Capacity Without Demand

A new plant starts producing before the market is ready to absorb its output. This creates a dangerous window where:

- **Inventory accumulates** faster than sales can clear it
- **Cash flow pressure** mounts as production costs run ahead of revenue
- **Quality perception suffers** if product is pushed into channels that aren't ready for it

The solution isn't to slow down production — that defeats the economics. The solution is to **build demand infrastructure in parallel with plant commissioning**.

## The Playbook

### 1. Product Mix Before Volume

Not all MDF is the same. Plain MDF, pre-laminated panels, HDF, moisture-resistant variants, and value-added products serve different market segments at different price points. Getting the product mix right from day one determines whether you're building margin or just moving volume.

At Archit Nuwood, we focused on:
- **Plain MDF** for the converter and furniture OEM segment (volume driver)
- **Pre-laminated panels** for the retail and project segment (margin driver)
- **Specialty grades** (moisture-resistant, fire-retardant) for institutional specifications

### 2. Specification-Led Demand

Volume through trade channels is necessary but insufficient. The sustainable demand comes from getting products **specified in projects** — government housing, institutional buildings, commercial interiors.

This required parallel workstreams:
- Product testing and certification (BIS, FSC, CARB compliance)
- Engagement with architects, PMCs, and government specification bodies
- Registration on procurement platforms and approved vendor lists

### 3. Distribution Before Full Capacity

We built the distributor and dealer network while the plant was still ramping up. The goal: **have channels ready to absorb output before production hits full capacity**.

This meant:
- Identifying and appointing distributors in key markets
- Training converter partners on product applications
- Establishing pricing structures and credit terms that incentivize early adoption

### 4. Plant-to-Market Protocols

The operational link between factory and market is often where greenfield launches fail. We established clear protocols for:

- **Order-to-dispatch cycle times** — ensuring reliable delivery
- **Quality control checkpoints** — before product reaches the market
- **Feedback loops** — market intelligence flowing back to production planning

## What I'd Do Differently

Every greenfield teaches you something. Key lessons:

1. **Start specification marketing 12 months before first dispatch** — the institutional sales cycle is long
2. **Over-invest in quality in the first year** — early reputation is disproportionately important
3. **Build the commercial team before the plant is ready** — don't wait for production to start hiring sales people

The companies that get greenfield commercialization right don't just fill capacity — they build a market position that compounds over years.
